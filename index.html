Social Media Usage and Mental Health : Insights from a Survey Experiment

Introduction

What factors influence our usage of technology, and how might this be effecting us?

As much of our lives today are spent online, it is hard to escape staring a screen. Although essential to our daily lives, is this prolonged use taking a toll on our mental health?

  • Prior research shows us that there is a positive correlation between time spent using technology and an increase in stress levels and anxiety.
  • The primary focus of this experiment was to to more specifically investigate the effects and factors of social media use.

Motivation

With the mental health crisis growing and becoming more prevalent, it is important to understand what may be contributing to this problem, and potentially identify ways to help alleviate it. While extensive research has been done in this area, there is still more to understand regarding the effects specifically of social media, rather than screens and electronics in general.

This study will focus on two main research questions:

  • What social factors might be associated with greater social media usage?
  • What psychological effects (mental health outcomes) correlate with increased social media engagement?

Introduction to Data

  • This data was collected from a survey experiment involving a random sample of 266 participants and was administered online in December 2024.
  • Survey questions were created according to current best practices, and results were weighed in an effort to make the sample demographic profile be more proportional to the current population demographics
  • “Social Media”, in this experiment, was defined as any online platform used to interact or communicate with others.

data <- data.frame(
  Meaningfulness = c("Extremely Meaningful", "Very Meaningful", "Somewhat Meaningful", 
                     "Not very Meaningful", "Not at all Meaningful"),
  Constantly = c(43, 33, 16, 5, 3),
  SeveralTimesDay = c(31, 44, 19, 4, 2),
  OnceDay = c(28, 39, 31, 0, 3),
  SeveralTimesWeek = c(14, 43, 21, 14, 7),
  OnceWeek = c(53, 13, 33, 0, 0),
  Never = c(33, 33, 22, 11, 0)
)
long_data <- pivot_longer(data, 
                          cols = -Meaningfulness, 
                          names_to = "Frequency", 
                          values_to = "Count")
long_data <- long_data %>%
  mutate(Category = case_when(
    Meaningfulness %in% c("Extremely Meaningful", "Very Meaningful", "Somewhat Meaningful") ~ "More Meaningful",
    TRUE ~ "Less Meaningful"
  ))
long_data <- long_data %>%
  group_by(Frequency) %>%
  mutate(Percent = Count / sum(Count) * 100) %>%
  ungroup()


summary_data <- long_data %>%
  group_by(Frequency, Category) %>%
  summarise(Percent = sum(Percent), .groups = "drop") %>%
  mutate(Percent = ifelse(Category == "Less Meaningful", -Percent, Percent))

summary_data$Frequency <- factor(summary_data$Frequency, levels = c(
  "Never", 
  "OnceWeek", 
  "SeveralTimesWeek", 
  "OnceDay", 
  "SeveralTimesDay", 
  "Constantly"
))
ggplot(summary_data, aes(x = Frequency, y = Percent, fill = Category)) +

Conclusions and Future Work

While this survey experiment exploring social media use and its potential correlations did not yield significant results, it still contributes to the overall understanding of this important topic. It is important to remember that finding a lack of significance can still be important to know. While the variables in this study were not significantly correlated, that does not mean that other similar factors may not be.

Further research could be conducted in this area, as there are countless other factors that could be examined in relationship to social media use. As this experiment looked at social media as a whole, additional studies might look into the significance of more explicit factors, such as specific social media platforms and mental health.

This survey experiment was also limited in terms of sample size, as this research could be replicated with a larger random sample to test for significance.
